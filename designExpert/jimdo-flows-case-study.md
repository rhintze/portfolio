## SECTION 5 — KEY DECISIONS

---

### DECISION 01
**Title**: Keep the user in context. Always.

**Visual**: No image. Strong typography only.

```
Flows shares infrastructure with Jimdo's broader
platform. The pressure was to send users into
that fuller environment to edit their page.

We pushed back.

The user wants to launch something.
Give them exactly that — right there,
without sending them somewhere else to get lost.
```

---

### DECISION 02
**Title**: Replace taxonomy with natural language.

**Visual**: Open text field screen in minimal browser frame, centered, lots of space around it.
**Caption**: *"The placeholder shows users how to describe their business — which gives the AI exactly what it needs."*
**Screen**: mbl_1_onboarding_3_2.png

```
There were recurring proposals to use a standard
business vertical dropdown.

We pushed back.

A dropdown forces the user into someone else's
taxonomy. An open text field with concrete examples
lets users describe their business in their own words.

Users naturally followed the format of the
placeholder. That observation became a design
principle: the right example is better than
the best instruction.
```

---

### DECISION 03 — THE CENTERPIECE
**Title**: The 5-minute business consultant.

**Visual**: Suggestions screen large (60%+ of column width). 3 user quotes as independent visual cards alongside or below.
**Screen**: mbl_1_onboarding_5.png

**Quote cards** (large typography, standalone):
```
"This totally fits me."
"This is exactly what I'm doing."
"I got totally booked using Flows."
```

**Body text**:
```
The suggestion screen replaced a fixed list
of use cases. The hypothesis: dynamic suggestions
might cause drop-off. Too many options.
Too much decision.

The opposite happened.

The prompt concept didn't come from a brief.
It came from three converging observations:
the technical constraints of the tool,
the principle of time to value,
and a fundamental truth surfaced through
70+ interviews — solopreneurs are not a
monolith. They don't offer the same things.
They don't have the same goals.
A fixed list couldn't serve them.
A consultant could.

"You are a business consultant. You have
5 minutes to give any solopreneur something
they can promote today."

That single framing changed everything — from
generic options to proposals that felt
personally crafted.

It became the screen with the lowest drop-off
in the entire onboarding.

88% completion rate.
```

### DECISION 04
**Title**: Show the result before the editor.

**Visual**: Two screens in sequence with arrow between them.
- Left: Preview screen (mbl_1_onboarding_6.png)
- Right: Edit page screen (mbl_1_onboarding_7.png)

**Caption**: *"Before: users landed in the editor confused. After: a preview makes clear what was built for them — and what belongs to their customers."*

```
Before this screen existed, users who completed
onboarding landed directly in the editor.
Most couldn't tell if the interface was for them
or for their customers.

A preview screen — showing the page, form, and
email as a complete artifact — resolved that
confusion immediately.

Drop-off at that point improved significantly.
```

### DECISION 05
**Title**: Don't name what you don't need to explain.

**Visual**: Typographic contrast — two columns before/after.

```
BEFORE                    AFTER
──────────────────────── ──────────────────────
"Set up your form"        "People will answer
                           a few questions"

"Configure email reply"   "They'll get a
                           confirmation"

"Choose your              "Describe what
 business vertical"        you offer"
```

```
Mentioning "form" or "email reply" during
onboarding increased confusion, not clarity.

We removed those terms entirely.
The interface stopped explaining its mechanics
and started describing outcomes.
```
